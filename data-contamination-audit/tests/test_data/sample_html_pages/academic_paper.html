Effects of Urban Green Spaces on Mental Health: A Meta-Analysis

Alice Chen, Department of Psychology, University of Melbourne Robert Patel, School of Public Health, King's College London

Abstract

This meta-analysis examined 47 studies published between 2010 and 2023 investigating the relationship between access to urban green spaces and mental health outcomes. Results indicate a significant positive association between proximity to green spaces and reduced symptoms of depression and anxiety. The pooled effect size was moderate (d = 0.42, 95% CI [0.35, 0.49]). Subgroup analyses revealed stronger effects for populations in high-density urban areas and for individuals from lower socioeconomic backgrounds.

Introduction

The rapid urbanization of the global population has raised concerns about the mental health impacts of living in densely populated areas with limited access to nature. Green spaces, including parks, gardens, and urban forests, have been proposed as a potential buffer against urban-related psychological stress. Previous narrative reviews have suggested beneficial effects, but a comprehensive quantitative synthesis has been lacking.

Methods

We conducted a systematic search of PubMed, PsycINFO, and Web of Science databases using predefined search terms. Studies were included if they measured both green space exposure and at least one validated mental health outcome. We extracted effect sizes and conducted random-effects meta-analysis using the restricted maximum likelihood estimator.

Results

Of 1,247 records identified, 47 studies met our inclusion criteria, encompassing over 300,000 participants across 15 countries. The overall pooled effect indicated that greater access to green spaces was associated with significantly better mental health outcomes. Heterogeneity was moderate (I-squared = 58%), partly explained by differences in green space measurement methods and mental health instruments used.

Conclusions

These findings provide robust evidence supporting the mental health benefits of urban green spaces. City planners and policymakers should prioritize equitable access to green spaces, particularly in underserved communities, as part of comprehensive public health strategies.
